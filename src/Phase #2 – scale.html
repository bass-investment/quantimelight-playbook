          Phase #2 – scale
          •   24 Minutes to read 
            Article Summary
            Share feedback    
             
            Thanks for sharing your feedback!
            The first stage was about experimentation and testing – now that you’ve identified your priorities (and got your payback period down to at most 18 months) you can start
            • building the infrastructure to sell at scale (people, processes, and tooling), and
            • testing new B2B marketing channels (performance marketing, affiliates, etc.).
            Building on the takeaways from the first phase – where you learnt about the most promising segments for your product – you can now start gradual and data-driven scaling in the segments and verticals with the best ROI and product market fit.
            Take advantage of the lessons learned at Revolut – save capital, and avoid having to let go complete sales teams due to overhiring or you growing the wrong verticals.

            Use KPIs to scale sales teams
            In order to avoid over-hiring, sales teams are scaled in three distinct phases, governed by their ability to generate ROI. Before scaling the team, it’s important to understand the verticals and segments you want to organise your teams around and what their potential maximum size at scale could be. To do this, the company followed these steps:
            • Step 1 – Set up teams by vertical
            • Step 2 – Calculate the “Size Potential” for your teams
            • Step 3 - Grow each team up to its potential in three hiring phases
            The key take-away about hiring for your sales team is to avoid adding too many people too fast to your team by monitoring some metrics to decide when and how many more people to hire.
            Step 1 – Set up teams by vertical
            You should use the data accumulated during Phase 1 to identify which segments delivered the highest ROI and build teams to target them. As your teams scale, you will gradually move towards lower ROI segments.
            There isn't a right way to split segments – this will be driven by the nature of your product, and by customer feedback during Phase 1. Revolut structured its segments by country (e.g. France, USA, Germany etc), but other typical B2B segmentation strategies are by company size (SMB, Mid-market, Enterprise) or industry (Health, Government, Financial Institutions etc).
            Example – which teams should I prioritise building?
            Let’s assume that, during Phase 1, you realised that your product has amazing product market fit with small companies, especially in South East Asia and the US, but poor traction with large companies.
            You will likely want to split your teams by country and start with teams in the US (Eastern, Western, South) and key SEA countries (Singapore, Thailand, Philippines).
            Step 2 – Calculate the Size Potential for your teams
            In order to pace your scaling effort correctly, you need to have a rough idea of how big each team should be at 'full maturity'. To do this, calculate the potential for growth for each segment team in terms of full-time equivalents (FTEs).
            Do this by benchmarking sales teams of mature competitors in markets and segments similar to yours. The following example illustrates the idea.
            Example for benchmarking potential team size
            Let’s assume you’re an accounting software provider for legal firms. Your target segment is UK SMBs.

            Step 3 – Grow teams in three phases
            Stretch out scaling into three phases to prevent hiring surges that result in overhiring, and having to let people go every six months – to make sure that scaling is gradual:
            • hire people in batches, not in bulk,
            • hire only for roles that make a return, and
            • respect the size potentials set out for each phase.
            KPIs to follow
            Monitor the following metrics while scaling, and use them as cutoffs to decide when to move into the next hiring phase:
            MetricExplanation
            Payback period
            An indicator of good ROI.
            Outbound gross profit
            Outbound sales requires actively pursuing leads, and indicates the need for more account execs.
            Gross profit generated in the team’s target segment
            This indicates that the segment/vertical has potential to grow.
            Time limits for each phase
            Setting clear deadlines for each phase creates urgency and gives you a clear time frame to consider downsizing the team or working on your product-market fit.
            Three stages of build-up
            1. Pilot: this is where you start out in phase 1 – focus on finding ROI and confirming product market fit for up to a year, before you further expand the team.
            2. Buildup: This is the start of phase two of the sales journey. Start hiring up to half your segment teams' potential when
              • the payback period at most 18 months;
              • and/or more than 50% of total gross profit is outbound;
              • and/or more than 70% of total gross profit is from the team’s target segment.
            3. Scale: If the ROI and the GP in the target segment improves even after you’ve started focusing on hiring, keep monitoring the metrics, and gradually grow the team further until you reach a steady state.

            When teams fall short of KPIs
            In case of a performance drop in a segment/vertical sales team (such as missing one or more of the goals set out for ROI, segment gross profit or outbound gross profit), don't panic.
            Before you jump to conclusions, look at the time limit set for the phase the team is in:
            Before Time Limit
            If you’re still within the recommended period for the current hiring phase
            Wait, and re-assess team performance at the end of the phase cutoff.
            Take this advice with the qualification that if underperformance is caused by high turnover and/or low morale within the team, you want to take prompt action.
            After Time Limit
            When you’re past the recommended time period for the current hiring phase
            Investigate, and see which fix works best in your case:
            • improve the product/product market fit – longer sales cycles or low customer retention are typical indicators of poor product market fit;
            • downsize the team;
            • close the team – You can re-start the team once you manage to improve product market fit.
            For example, when a European company’s sales team in a small Asian market has been failing to reach its targets over the past two years, that’s a clear indication of poor product market fit. The team in this scenario should be closed down, and not re-started until product market fit improves.
            Closing down underperforming teams
            In markets above a certain size, it isn’t best practice to shut down even poorly-performing sales teams completely.
            For example, a European company establishing US presence would likely keep its US sales team even if it's struggling, because it’s a strategically important market.
            Hiring and retaining AEs
            One of the main blockers to successfully growing your sales team is going to be the sourcing and retention of good talent. At Revolut, AE churn became so high at one time that hiring resources were mostly spent on maintaining rather than growing the team.
            Developing a churn-proof sales AE hiring process goes a long way to prevent having to backfill your sales positions while trying to expand the team.
            In Revolut’s experience, most sales account executives (AE) leave in the first six months in the role – with poor performance accounting for an overwhelming majority (more than 90%) of exits.
            Sales AE churn at Revolut used to be threatening to bring the department to a standstill: it peaked at 7–8% a month, which would be like having to re-hire your entire team every year.
            What is a healthy long-term target for churn?
            There will always be some churn – you shouldn’t worry about a 2–3% of monthly churn among average performers.
            What you’ll want to focus on is keeping all of your top performers. Track churn separately for employees who have been rated strong in two consecutive quarters, and aims to keep churn as close to 0% as possible with special rewards:
            • exponential monetary rewards for overachievement of quotas,
            • mandatory salary review or consideration for promotion after 2 quarters of strong performance,
            • depending on the individual or the regional culture, recognition programs, involvement in special projects, or the opportunity to become a certified interviewer can be a top-performer incentive.
            Although Revolut doesn’t currently have this, you might also consider setting up an exclusive "president's" or "champion's" club for exceptional salespeople, with five-star trips as rewards.
            The two greatest contributing factors to performance-related churn are hiring and training.
            ProblemSolutions
            Hiring
            Sales teams lower the talent bar to meet growth expectations
            • Expand screening channels, and accept candidates year-round
            • Standardise interview processes to maintain talent bar
            • Perfect onboarding: provide as many resources as possible at offer stage to ensure top candidate convert
            Training
            Poor training for probationary AEs, both on the job and with learning resources
            • Use learning-focused KPIs to promote on-the-job training
            • Set up a learning programme for both probationary and seasoned AEs
            A note about assessing sales AE performance
            AE performance is assessed based on hitting sales quotas. Some sample quota performance cut-offs:
            Quota fulfillmentPerformance bucketNote
            80–120%Average
            >120%Strong
            Around 20% of sales AEs at any one time are in the Exceptional bucket.
            For a Strong or Exceptional rating, AEs also need to demonstrate alignment with company values.
            >150%Exceptional
            Expand screening
            A bigger talent pool means more high-quality talent to pick.
            • Don’t only use LinkedIn to look for candidates, but try all regionally relevant job boards.
            • Spread hiring targets over the year to avoid recruiter capacity bottlenecks.
            • Add channels for candidates to proactively apply for sales positions:
              • Referrals
              • Graduate outreach programme, with on-the-job training
            Standardise sales interviewing
            See the hiring resources in a dedicated section of this playbook, and the hiring playbook for details on hiring practices in general.
            Standardising interviews prevents sales teams from lowering the talent bar when they hire, and it improves hiring precision.
             You should map out in detail the number, focus, and even contents of sales hiring interviews. You can take a look at the example of a sales interview process on the Sales hiring resources page. However, if you are thinking about adapting it to your process, the key design principles are:
            • Skills-focused interviewing. Avoid hiring the wrong candidates by interviewing for the same skills that AEs are evaluated on on the job.
              • Create competency matrix of skills each AE needs per seniority
              • Design interview process to match competency matrix
            • Standardisation. Keep the talent bar high by standardising questions and scorecards in playbooks for each interview type.
            • Interviewer certification. Maintain your interviewers' standards by enforcing regular re-certification for them.
            About interviewer certification
            To ensure a consistently high talent bar across the organisation, in-house certification for the specific type of interview should be a prerequisite for interviewers to conduct interviews.
            Certification should be limited to employees experienced in the relevant domain, and involve training, assessment, and continuous monitoring.
            In a nutshell, certified interviewers must follow a playbook with set questions to conduct interviews, and evaluate interviewees based on standardised scorecards. Whenever the playbook or the scorecard is updated, interviewers need to be re-certified.
            Read more about the hiring interviewer certification programme in the Hiring playbook.
            There are three interviews in the sales hiring process, with the following pattern:
            Interview stepDescription
            #1 – Sales skills interview
            Hypothetical examples testing core sales skills and business acumen – think sales-focused problem-solving, negotiation, and business case development.
            #2 – Selling experience interview
            Discussing past experience, asking about one or more difficult deals the candidate has managed to set up.
            #3 – Values and achievement assessment
            A senior sales lead assesses the candidate’s aspirations, motivations, and sales team fit.
            Motivate during onboarding
            Use onboarding as a tool to improve the ratio of accepted sales AE offers.
            • Prepare content to help close sales AE candidates, with topics such as
              • what they can expect doing the job (“a typical day of a sales AE”), and
              • how and when their commission is calculated.
            • Make it standard practice for recruiters to answer candidates' questions in dedicated closing calls.
            • Organise intro calls for candidates with established AEs to inform and inspire.
            By treating onboarding as “pre-probation”, you’ll’ll get a better hit rate on top talent.
            Use learning-oriented KPIs for probation
            Once their probation ends, sales AEs earn their commission based on gross profit quotas – however, in order for probationary sales AEs to pick up as much as possible during their first months, they have different KPIs to motivate them and encourage learning on the job.
            Instead of GP quotas, which are of course still monitored, it's better to measure the performance of probationary sales on
            • Inbound-focused sales quotas
               Inbound leads have a much higher potential conversion rate. This keeps the sales rep motivated, helps them ramp up to become a seasoned AE, and drives conversion.
            • Conversion ratios
               Removing the size of the client (that is, their contribution to GP) is like “bicycle stabilisers” for the AE, motivating them to focus on their “batting average” while they practice their role.
            Overhaul training process
            Develop and distribute in-house training materials to ensure consistent messaging, and to enhance sales skills such as negotiation or objection handling.
            Consider the following three areas for training materials:
            • Cheat sheets to help short-circuit learning about things like
              • tooling (e.g. Salesforce), and
              • key processes (e.g. objection handling, prospecting, closing)
            • Training sessions. In-house training covers the following four macro topics:
              • Intro and Tooling: the main technical tools for the job (e.g. Salesforce, and LinkedIn Sales Navigator), and information on key processes (such as sales escalation, and performance management)
              • Product Knowledge: in-depth info sessions for each product you offer, with explicit tips on how to sell each product or feature
              • Sales skills: workshops on the company’s sales strategy and approach (including how-tos on objection handling, product demos, outreach, and closing), and “level-up” sessions with advanced sales tecniques (the MEDDICC sales framework)
              • Risk and compliance: this is only relevant for companies in regulated industries
            • Mentoring
               Help new joiner salespeople get up-to-speed faster by pairing them with a more experienced “buddy” to answer their job-related questions for their first 45 days on the job. The mentor and the new joiner shadow each other’s client sessions and discuss takeaways to help transfer best practices.
            Diversify teams
            At the start of your sales journey, you didn’t know enough about product market fit to have specialised sales team members.
            Now, you’ll still focus mostly on hiring account executives, because the overwhelming majority of your sales teams should be about getting more business.
            From a single sales team sharing countries and verticals, focusing on finding product fit, your sales organisation is evolving into a structured set of segment teams, with new management layers. Some of this evolution is bound to be organic, such as some long-time AEs – with the right people skills and willingness – taking on more responsibility as managers.
            Promoting AEs to sales managers
            What makes a good SM
            Apart from people management and hiring, which is required for all managers, SMs are expected to be proficient in three skills
            • Sales Skills: SMs must be experts at selling, and willing to demonstrate their skills to be able to coach their AEs into rockstar salespeople, or close important deals.
            • Business Acumen: SMs are required to develop a data-driven and highly logical sales strategy for their team, and course-correct if things don’t go as planned, so the bar for the expected business acumen is well above a regular AE’s.
            • Data Analysis: data manipulation is key to both managing the performance of a large team, and monitoring and defining a sales strategy.
            Internal promotions
            Nurturing top talent by promoting your top AEs to SMs is best practice, but you’ll need an internal screening process, because not all great salespeople make great managers.
            Internal SM candidates go through a simplified hiring process of a minimum requirements bar, and two interviews.
            • The minimum requirements are that the candidate demonstrated strong sales skills during their as an AE, and has been able to successfully coach colleagues. You can quantify this with the following criteria:
              • Rated Strong in the past 2 quarters (equivalent to being top 10–20% for achievement)
              • Outbound-driven: over 70% of their contracts from outbound leads
              • Manager reviews proving
                • Coaching skills demonstrated with several peers
                • Process-driven, Sales/CRM hygiene
                • Good Culture fit (Company Values and Sales Dpt culture)
            • The Business Acumen and People Management interview: the goal of this interview is to assess strategy and management qualities.
               Past examples of special initiatives that the AE took part in demonstrating leadership are taken into account, but the gist of the interview is a case study where the candidate is supposed to
              • formulate a data-driven sales strategy for a market segment,
              • course-correct the strategy when given new information,
              • explain the size and makeup of the team of AEs they would hire to cover the market segment,
              • explain how they would handle and coach top performers, average performers and underperformers.
            • The Data Analysis Interview: AEs are not normally required to manipulate data, but this skill is essential for SMs. In this interview, candidates are shown detailed data of an underperforming team. Candidates are required to
              • identify the root causes of underperfomance, and
              • formulate a data-driven solution to improve the performance of the team (such as tweaking sales strategy, coaching average performers, or firing poor performers).
            When hiring Sales Managers, we recommend a similar process as the one for internal candidates, but Business Acumen and People Management are assessed through two separate, more in-depth, interviews. These interviews are still structured as a case study.
            Introducing ancillary roles
            A sales organisation requires more than salespeople. At this stage, start introducing small supporting teams. Initially staffed with 1–2 people, these teams will naturally grow as your company moves to Phase 3.
            Ancillary roleNote
            Sales Operations
            Sales Enablement
            Their goal is to create content and processes that make salespeople more efficient. Examples include
            • Training resources
            • Sales support materials (demos, pitch decks etc)
            Business Planning and Data Analysis
            Look after analytics (metrics, dashboards, KPI-setting and commission validation), lead scoring/marketing effectiveness analysis, and planning (define AE goals and quotas)
            Sales Tools & Sales Engineering
            This team is charged with:
            • Manage a high-performing CRM
            • Build and maintain auxiliary tools that improve sales performance, such as CRM analytics, lead collection and prospecting automation tools, AI-powered lead scoring, or quality control systems.
            What should your team look like for this?
            • Start with 2 people: 1 expert engineer that can take over the role of product owner, with a more junior engineer to support
            Revolut now has a 12-person team for a thousand AEs, made up mostly of engineers, with a couple of data scientists to implement AI-based solutions.
            Special Projects
            This is a team of operators (most often from a consulting background) in charge of helping build the core processes of your sales department and support the Head of Sales in case of special projects (such as setting up a feature tracker, or a customer feedback management system).
            Account ManagementWith the quick growth in phase two, you’re more likely to have fewer long-standing customers, so customer care and/or customer upselling isn’t a major issue.
            However, as you grow to your potential, customer churn will get increasingly relevant.
            Best practice is to set up a small account management team preemptively so you don’t have to scramble when you need it later.
            For reference, Revolut had a 5–6-member account management team at this stage.
            Product Sales SpecialistsSupport AEs with product knowledge for demos and when developing value propositions.
            This role isn’t relevant to all industries or products – you be the judge of whether you need it.
            OnboardingGuide new customers through the steps to start using your product, and collect their feedback
            You probably already have one or two people doing this since phase 1.
            Sales DevelopmentThis department generates and manages leads – identifying, qualifying, and getting in touch with potential customers.
            Revolut does not have sales development representatives (SDRs) but it’s a helpful role to have – see the note below.
            Due to the nature of Revolut’s product, and the complexity of the onboarding process, it makes more sense to have AEs guide prospects through the entire sales cycle.
            A note about sales development
            SDRs can improve the efficiency of AEs by booking meetings with prospects, and reaching out to outbound leads, and pre-qualifying inbound inquiries – then handing off the prospect to AEs.
            Best practice is to incentivise SDRs in two ways:
            1. Based on the number of “qualified leads” they create – to avoid racking up low-quality meetings, you’ll need a process for AEs to confirm that the prospect booked was a good fit.
            2. Commissions as a percentage of the annual recurring revenue (ARR) of the deals closed from the meetings they booked.
            You can also easily build an “internal sales talent pipeline” for well-performing SDRs to graduate to AEs.
            As for numbers, a common rule of thumb is to have 3 SDRs for every 10 AEs.
            Standardise processes
            We have already mentioned the sales onboarding and hiring processes – however, with a a scaled-up sales team, you’ll also need to assess and streamline the processes that affect seasoned sales AEs.
            Systematise sales enablement
            Create, broadcast, enforce, and maintain a central set of sales enablement resources for the department as a whole:
            • Call scripts: inbound, outbound, follow-up call, closing call, etc.
            • Demo scripts: product feature, technical deep-dive, use case demo, etc.
            • Prospecting guidelines: best practices for identifying, targeting, and engaging prospective clients or leads.
            • Email templates: introductory, meeting request, proposal, etc.
            • Objection handling: examples, guidance, further reading on various objection handling strategies: reframing, risk reversal, price justification, etc.
            • Demo accounts: to be used for product demos and sales presentations.
            • Recorded demos: ideally transcribed and time-stamped.
            • Technical Q&A: make sure to keep these up-to-date as features, and architecture potentially change.
            See the Sales enablement resources section of this playbook for some guidance on these.
            If you run into trouble developing some of these resources, reach out to your QuantumLight representative for an expert session or further materials.
            Make the most of your tech stack
            Customer relations management tools (CRMs) typically offer activity and data analysis, and dashboarding features. Revolut uses the monitoring and issue tracking features of Salesforce, its CRM of choice, and the platform’s Salesforce Analytics BI tool to monitor metrics in three macro areas:
            Macro areaMetrics
            General performance
            Monitor the following metrics to identify areas for improvement, and make data-driven decisions to optimise strategies
            • Overall Gross profit
            • Overall Sales profit (Gross profit minus (AE salary + commission))
            • ROI projection
            • Gross profit by business by lead type (inbound, outbound, and backbook)
            • Customer churn by lead type (inbound, outbound, and backbook)
            Salesforce dynamics
            Identify retention trends and gauge hiring pressure (see the example dashboard)
            • Number of AEs by tenure (0–3 months, 4–12 months, 12+ months)
            • AE churn by tenure (0–3 months, 4–12 months, 12+ months)
            • Sales hiring pipeline
            Sales productivity
             Assess the profitability of your sales department
            • Gross profit per AE by tenure (0–3 months, 4–12 months, 12+ months)
            • Sales Profit (Gross profit minus (AE salary + commission)) per AE by tenure (0–3 months, 4–12 months, 12+ months)
            • Conversion rates by lead type (inbound, outbound, and backbook)
            • Average gross profit by lead type (inbound, outbound, and backbook)

            Try new marketing channels
            The phase when you’re scaling your sales team is well-suited to setting the foundations for long-term growth by diversifying your go-to-market strategy.
            Test the waters with alternative marketing channels – at this stage, your goal isn’t generating revenue yet, but collecting data for a later expansion, much like you did for sales in phase one.
            • Do small-scale experiments with a variety of marketing channels.
               Focus on learning about how they work for you – you’ll use these lessons in the next phase when allocating budget between them.
            • Don’t ramp up marketing channel spend at this stage.
            • Hire a execution-oriented person for your channel experiments.
               Look for someone with enough experience to grow into the head of the channel when you decide to scale, but not so senior that they can’t independently execute and stay close to the details.
            Marketing channels to try
            Revolut’s best early marketing channel experiment results were with Google Ads performance marketing – it offers good ROI for many business models, and you can scale your investment up or down easily.
            After its successful performance marketing experiments, Revolut tried affiliates, partnerships, and brand marketing.
            Key learnings are
            • (Strategic) partnerships aren’t recommended at this stage of your sales journey:
              • Difficult to scale.
              • As an early-stage start-up with limited leverage, you’ll likely be negotiated out of the business benefit by the marketing partner.
              • Bigger companies you partner with are likely to have several marketing partners apart from you, which may have you competing for visibility, dilute your brand, or mean a conflict of interest.
            • Brand marketing doesn’t work at this stage – it’s a relatively large investment, and prohibitively difficult to correctly attribute its conversion rates.
            Don’t walk away from partnerships altogether.
            Your mileage may vary with partnerships depending on the industry or the nature of your business, and smaller-scale partnerships (such as with brokers or accountants) can still work well even at this stage.
            Metrics to focus on
            While ROI is the most important factor of all, the following are aspects that will also influence your marketing budget decisions later.
            • Speed. How fast can you set up a campaign in the specific channel?
            • Scalability. At what level of investment does the channel hit diminishing returns, when further budget allocation shrinks or flips ROI?
            • Data integration. How essential is it to add data back-end resources to make this channel work?
            • Cost – upfront and ongoing. With performance marketing in particular, focus on determining a baseline customer acquisition cost (CAC) as early as possible. Although this metric tends to fluctuate with time, it will help you determine initial budget allocation for the channel later.
            Moving on to phase three
            Good indications for wrapping up sales team scaling, and re-focusing on complementing your sales with other B2B channels for further growth:
            • You’re down to a 12-month payback period.
            • Your topline growth has stabilised – ideally, you’ll want to anticipate the growth plateau, and start budgeting B2B channels a quarter or two before it happens.
